About Me

I’m a game designer with an engineering background, currently working as a Game Design Lecturer. I focus on teaching systems-driven game design and player experience, guiding students through hands-on projects that explore mechanics, progression, and interactive worlds, while drawing on practical development knowledge to ensure ideas translate effectively into playable games.

My work blends technical systems design with a strong interest in character, narrative, and player engagement. Informed by advanced research into how people connect with fictional characters, I approach design as both a creative and analytical practice - crafting games that feel intentional, emotionally resonant, and rewarding to play.

Games That Inspire Me

Below are some games that inspire me and influence the way that I think about and design games

Hades (Supergiant Games)

Hades reshaped how I think about failure, showing how repeated play can feel narratively and mechanically rewarding rather than punishing.

Key Takeaways:

  • Failure as progression, not reset
  • Narrative persistence across runs
  • Systems that reinforce theme and character
  • Exceptional readbility in fast, chaotic combat
  • Player motivation driven by curiosity, not grind

Florence (Mountains)

Florence demonstrates how interaction itself can carry emotional weight, using simple mechanics to communicate complex human experiences.

Key Takeaways:

  • Mechanics as emotional storytelling tools
  • UI interactions that reflect character states
  • Minimalism used intentionally, not sparingly
  • Narrative conveyed through play, not exposition
  • Strong focus on empathy and player reflection

Azul (Michael Kiesling)

Azul highlights the elegance of well-designed systems, showing how simple rules can produce deep strategy and meaningful player interaction.

Key Takeaways:

  • Simple rulesets with emergent complexity
  • Clear information design and game state readbility
  • Indirect player interaction and strategic tension
  • Strong balance between planning and adaptability
  • Systems-first thinking, independent of medium

Dead Cells (Motion Twin)

Dead Cells reinforces the importance of mechanical clarity and responsiveness, where mastery comes from player skill rather than hidden systems.

Key Takeaways:

  • Tight expressive combat and movement
  • Clear cause-and-effect feedback
  • Difficulty that feels fair through consistency
  • Encourages learning through repetition
  • Mechanical depth without over-explanation
